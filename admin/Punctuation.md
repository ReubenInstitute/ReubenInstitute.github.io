# Punctuation

Modern punctuation symbols are applied at positions roughly corresponding to cantillation marks, maintaining their grammatical functions.

The etnaḥta (major verse pause) is rendered as a semicolon when the two halves form a coordinate compound sentence. When the relationship is explanatory, consequential, conditional, summative, or otherwise non‑coordinate, the semicolon is replaced with the appropriate punctuation (em dash, colon, comma, etc.) as determined by the logical function.

| Punctuation Mark | Rule |
|---|---|
| Comma | for brief pauses within phrases |
| Colon | introducing direct speech, lists, or explanations |
| Period | end of sentence, except at end of poetic verses |
| Exclamation mark | indicating strong emotion or commands |
| Double exclamation mark | expressing extreme emphasis or astonishment |
| Question mark | ending direct inquiries |
| Combined ?! | showing rhetorical questions or wonderings |
| Double curled quotation marks (" ") | setting off direct speech or quotations |
| Single curled quotation marks (' ') | highlighting special terms or phrases |
| Em dash | used to mark a strong logical break where the second part explains, specifies, results from, or sums up the first, such as explanatory, consequential, conditional, summative, or identifying. It is attached to the preceding word and followed by a space. The em dash is used only when the break has strong added value; otherwise, a comma is preferred. |

## Speech Formatting

**Inline Speech**
Inline speech integrated within narrative sentences is enclosed in quotation marks. No em dash or colon is used before the speech. A colon before the quotation is used only when the quoted material is a complete statement; otherwise, no colon is used.

**Block Speech**
When speech is presented on a new line, the preceding line ends with a colon. The speech itself begins on a new line with an em dash (—) and is enclosed in quotation marks.

**Multi-Paragraph Speech**
Speech that spans multiple paragraphs begins each paragraph with an em dash (—) and is enclosed in quotation marks. The quotation marks are closed at the interruption and reopened in the new paragraph.

**Nested Speech**
Quotes or thoughts within other speech are enclosed in single quotation marks.

**Mixed Narration and Speech (Verse/Passage)**
In single verses or short passages where narration and speech alternate line by line, speech lines begin with an em dash (—) and are enclosed in quotation marks. Narration lines are not preceded by an em dash. Inline speech within narration lines remains enclosed in quotation marks.

**Cold-Called Speech**
Cold-called speech (spoken or thought without a preceding attribution phrase) is formatted like block speech. It begins on a new line with an em dash (—) and is enclosed in quotation marks.

**Use of ?!**
The combined question mark and exclamation point (?!) is used only in cases of intense emotional rhetorical questions where a standard question mark is insufficient to convey the full emotional force. The surrounding narrative context must clearly indicate that the question is not a genuine inquiry. This mark is applied sparingly and deliberately.

## Non‑Poetic Elements

Instructions and non-poetic elements, such as psalm titles (e.g., "מזמור לדוד", "למנצח") and the term "סֶלָה" (Selah), must be rendered in bold and on separate lines. These elements are considered external or performance/liturgical instructions, not integral parts of the poetic text itself.

## Divine Name

In non-biblical texts, the divine name must be written יהוה and not ה or יי and without diacritics.
