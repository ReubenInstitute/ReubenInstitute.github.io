# Aramaic Class

This Aramaic language course is designed for subscription-based social media platforms, providing a highly structured, performance-oriented curriculum for digital learners. The method uses a scripted dialogue between a narrator and a cast of figurines to teach grammar through spatial logic and physical action. Instead of traditional prose explanations, the course utilizes high-density tables that link Hebrew script and phonetic IPA targets directly to pointing actions. This approach allows subscribers to master complex linguistic patterns—such as gender agreement and glottal shifts—through visual mimicry and repetition.

## Curriculum

1. **Class 1: Nouns** – Basic objects and people to establish environment and gender patterns.
2. **Class 2: Adjectives** – Descriptive words to practice gender and number agreement.
3. **Class 3: Numbers** – Counting nouns using masculine and feminine cardinal numbers.
4. **Class 4: Pronouns** – Subject pronouns for identifying actors in a sentence.
5. **Class 6: Verbs** – Present tense actions to animate the figurines and create scenes.
6. **Class 6: Prepositions & Particles** – Connectors to build complex thoughts and relationships.

### Individual classes

* Basic Vowels (Pataḥ, Qamaṣ, Segol, Ṣere, Ḥiriq, Qibbuṣ, Ḥolam, Shva)
* Reduced Vowels (Ḥatafs) (Ḥataf Pataḥ, Ḥataf Qamaṣ, Ḥataf Segol)
* Begadkefat Pairs (בּ/ב, גּ/ג, דּ/ד, כּ/כ, פּ/פ, תּ/ת)
* Guttural Pairs (א vs. ע, ה vs. ח, כּ/כ vs. ק)

## Audio

Each line is recorded, spoken in authentic Eastern Aramaic pronunciation, then cloned into a set of seven distinct voices using ElevenLabs: one pedagogical Professor voice, three male Teaching Assistant voices, and three female Teaching Assistant voices. For singular demonstrations, the same original recording is cloned with the relevant TA voice. For plural groups, each of the originals (recorded with slightly different pacing or intonation) is cloned using a different voice from the same gender set. The resulting MP3 files are assembled directly into videos via FFmpeg without any post‑processing or effects.

## Visuals

Professor shots feature only a forearm and hand holding a pointing stick, seen against a traditional green chalkboard. Chalk‑written Aramaic words appear on the board as the stick points to each word, creating a clean, focused instructional frame separate from the figurines’ world.

### The Teaching Assistants

The Teaching Assistants serve as the active demonstrators of grammatical concepts, operating in a pedagogical space separate from the professor's instructional frame. They exist to physically enact linguistic rules through clear, scripted actions and dialogue. All implementations feature unambiguous male and female visual forms to demonstrate grammatical gender, paired with distinct male and female voice actors for auditory reinforcement. The voices have multiple plural versions: male-only groups, female-only groups, and mixed-gender groups, allowing comprehensive demonstration of number and gender agreement. In non-photorealistic renderings, facial features remain absent by default, appearing only when directly relevant to the vocabulary being taught—for example, a mouth materializing only during speech verbs, ears during listening, or a nose during smelling—ensuring visual focus remains on the linguistic lesson rather than decorative anatomy.

#### Option: Figurines

The figurines are gender-differentiated plasteline forms (feminine with long hair and skirt, masculine plain) with no permanent facial features. Facial elements appear only when grammatically necessary—a mouth for speaking or eating, nose holes for smelling, ear openings for hearing—making anatomy a direct visual cue for related verbs. Limbs end in softly rounded cuts, and while their chubby forms suggest deliberate movement, they are capable of agile actions as required. Roles are indicated by minimal props (e.g., crown, hat), and all objects share the same dough-like material with soft geometry and muted textures, maintaining a sterile, washed-out environment that keeps focus on the language task.

#### Option: La Linea

Rendered as minimalist white line drawings on a black background, evoking the iconic La Linea animation style. Each figure is defined by a continuous contour line that distinguishes gender through silhouette—feminine forms indicated by hair and skirt lines, masculine by simpler outlines. Facial features (mouth, ears, nose) are absent by default and are drawn in only when grammatically triggered by vocabulary such as speaking, hearing, or smelling. In portrait‑oriented layouts, singular figures appear centered; plural groups are represented by two or three stacked outlines rather than side‑by‑side arrangements, ensuring clarity within vertical video formats. All pointing, gesturing, and interaction are conveyed through the movement and extension of the line itself, while objects and scenery remain as static line‑art elements in the background.

#### Option: Photo‑Realistic

Presented as photo‑realistic AI‑generated figures grounded in the material culture of the ancient Near East. Their clothing is rendered with simple, textural accuracy—plain linen tunics, wool mantles, and leather sandals—avoiding ornate or anachronistic details. Facial expressions remain deliberately neutral and focused, conveying a pedagogical rather than dramatic or emotional presence. This approach prioritizes clarity and historical plausibility, allowing learners to observe grammatical actions—pointing, counting, interacting with objects—without visual distraction or stylistic exaggeration.


## Aramaic Phonetics

| Letter | Name | IPA | Description | Reference |
|--------|------|-----|-------------|-----------|
| א | **Alef** | [ʔ] | Glottal stop | As in "**A**frica" |
| בּ | **Bet (with dagesh)** | [b] | Voiced bilabial stop | Normal "b" sound |
| ב | **Vet (without dagesh)** | [β] | Voiced bilabial fricative | Softer than [v], like Amharic "mevlat" |
| גּ | **Gimel (with dagesh)** | [g] | Voiced velar stop | Normal "g" sound |
| ג | **Ghimel (without dagesh)** | [ɣ] | Voiced velar fricative | Arabic ghayn ("punctured g") |
| דּ | **Dalet (with dagesh)** | [d] | Voiced dental stop | Normal "d" sound |
| ד | **Dhalet (without dagesh)** | [ð] | Voiced dental fricative | As in "**th**is" |
| ה | **He** | [h] | Voiceless glottal fricative | Clear [h], stronger than Hungarian aspiration |
| ו | **Vav** | [ʋ] | Voiced labiodental approximant | Smooth frictionless sound between [v] and [u] |
| ז | **Zayin** | [z] | Voiced alveolar fricative | Normal "z" sound |
| ח | **Ḥet** | [ħ] | Voiceless pharyngeal fricative | "Angry Arabic" quality, back of throat |
| ט | **Tet** | [tˤ] | Emphatic voiceless dental stop | "Exploding t" with strong emphasis |
| י | **Yod** | [j] | Voiced palatal approximant | As in "**y**es" |
| כּ | **Kaf (with dagesh)** | [k] | Voiceless velar stop | Normal "k" sound |
| כ | **Khaf (without dagesh)** | [χ] | Voiceless uvular fricative | Vigorous "khhhhh" like Modern Hebrew ח |
| ל | **Lamed** | [l] | Voiced alveolar lateral approximant | Clear "l", not velarized |
| מ | **Mem** | [m] | Voiced bilabial nasal | Normal "m" sound |
| נ | **Nun** | [n] | Voiced alveolar nasal | Normal "n" sound |
| ס | **Samekh** | [t͡s] | Voiceless alveolar affricate | Single consonant like Italian "pizza" |
| ע | **Ayin** | [ʕ] | Voiced pharyngeal fricative | Deeper than modern Arabic, aggressive quality |
| פּ | **Pe (with dagesh)** | [p] | Voiceless bilabial stop | Normal "p" sound |
| פ | **Fe (without dagesh)** | [f] | Voiceless labiodental fricative | Normal "f" sound |
| צ | **Tsadi** | [sˤ] | Emphatic voiceless alveolar fricative | Hard emphatic "SS" (reversed from Modern Hebrew) |
| ק | **Qof** | [q] | Voiceless uvular stop | From back of throat but audible "c" sound |
| ר | **Resh** | [r] | Voiced alveolar trill | Deep, forward thrill like Romanian/Italian "R" |
| שׁ | **Shin** | [ʃ] | Voiceless postalveolar fricative | Normal "sh" sound |
| שׂ | **Sin** | [s] | Voiceless alveolar fricative | Normal "s" sound |
| תּ | **Tav (with dagesh)** | [t] | Voiceless dental stop | Normal "t" sound |
| ת | **Thav (without dagesh)** | [θ] | Voiceless dental fricative | As in "**th**ink" |
