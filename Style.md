# Style

## Psalm Descriptions

### Divine Name
* **English:** The deity is referred to as **God**.
* **Hebrew:** The deity is referred to as **אלוהים**.
* No other divine name (YHWH, Lord, Hashem, Adonai) appears in the descriptions, regardless of the specific name in the original Hebrew verse.

### Authorship
* When the superscription explicitly names an author, the description opens with the attribution.
* **English pattern:** “A psalm by David…” or the fact is woven into the narrative (e.g., “David describes…”).
* **Hebrew pattern:** **מזמור על ידי דויד** or a natural narrative opening that conveys the same (e.g., “דויד מתפלל ש…”).
* If the psalm is unattributed, authorship is simply omitted; the description starts directly with the content.

### Archaic Language & Technical Terms
* Ordinary language in the descriptions is **modern**, in both English and Hebrew.
* Unique, untranslatable terms that appear in the Hebrew text itself (Nefilim, Miktam, Maskil, keruvim, shofar, etc.) are **retained in transliteration** and **glossed briefly in parentheses** the first time they appear in a psalm description.
* Gloss structure:
  * **English:** `term (a “meaning,” from “root”—definition)`
  * **Hebrew:** `term ("משמעות", מלשון "שורש"—הגדרה)`
* Example (Miktam):
  * EN: a Miktam (a “golden” psalm, from “ketem”—fine gold)
  * HE: מכתם ("מזמור זהב", מלשון "כתם"—זהב טהור)
* Performance markers like *Selâh* are not explained in the description.

### Hebrew as Parallel Composition
* The Hebrew description carries the same information, tone, emotional arc, and scholarly framing as the English version.
* It is written in natural, modern Hebrew — not a word‑by‑word translation.
* Differences in word order, sentence length, and idiomatic phrasing are natural and expected.

### Overall Style
* **Voice:** Modern, calm, inviting, as if one knowledgeable guide is speaking in both languages.
* **Length:** Two to three sentences (or a similarly short paragraph).
* **Tense:** Present simple is used throughout.
* **Emotional arc:** Each description indicates where the psalm begins (distress, wonder, praise) and where it concludes (trust, hope, joy).
* **Quotations:** Direct biblical quotations and archaic vocabulary from the psalm’s text are avoided. Imagery is summarised in fresh, contemporary language.

### Production Sequence
* The **English description** is drafted and polished first.
* The **Hebrew description** is then composed to match all facts, nuances, and glosses, while expressing them in idiomatic Hebrew.
* Both versions are checked for consistent attribution, technical glosses, and tonal alignment.

## Modern Hebrew Translation

*(Complementary to source texts, not standalone)*  
The structure must be kept exactly as in the original verses.

### No Concatenated Possessives
Replace bound (possesive,etc) forms with analytic constructions.

For example:  
   - "פִּרְיוֹ" → **"הַפְּרִי שֶׁלּוֹ"**  
   - "בְּתוֹרָתוֹ" → **"בַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁלּוֹ"**  
   - "עָלָיו" → **"הֶעָלִים שֶׁלּוֹ"**  
   - "אדוני" → **"אדון שלי"**  
   - "אלוהי" → **"אלוה שלי"**  
   - סובבוני - סובבו אותי  
   - יבעתוני - יבעתו אותי  

#### Simple Prepositions & Comparisons
Modernize poetic/archaic relational terms.  

For example:  
- "כְּעֵץ" → **"כְּמוֹ עֵץ"**  
   - "מן הים" → **"מהים"**  

### Modern Vocabulary Only
Substitute archaic terms with contemporary equivalents, except if the archaic term has the exact same meaning in modern Hebrew.  
For example:  
- "עֵת" → **"זְמַן"**  
   - "כתמול שלשלום" → **"כמו אתמול שלשלום"**  

### Short Parenthetical Clarifications
Add ≤5-word explanations for ambiguous terms.  

For example:  
- "לֵצִים" → **"לֵצִים (אֲנָשִׁים מְזַלְזְלִים)"**  
   - "יָקֻמוּ" → **"יַעַמְדוּ (לֹא יִשָּׂאוּ אֶת הַדִּין)"**  

### Natural Verb Conjugations
Update verb forms to modern usage.  
For example:  
- "תֹּאכֵל" → **"תֹּאכַל"**  
   - "תֹּאבֵד" → **"תֹּאֲבַד"**  
   - "ויגיע" → **"הגיע"**  
   - וייכנס → נכנס

### Divine Name
The divine name must be kept as יהוה without diacritics, not shortened to ה or יי.

## Punctuation

Modern punctuation symbols are applied at positions roughly corresponding to cantillation marks, maintaining their grammatical functions.

The etnaḥta (major verse pause) is rendered as a semicolon when the two halves form a coordinate compound sentence. When the relationship is explanatory, consequential, conditional, summative, or otherwise non‑coordinate, the semicolon is replaced with the appropriate punctuation (em dash, colon, comma, etc.) as determined by the logical function.

| Punctuation Mark | Rule |
|---|---|
| Comma | for brief pauses within phrases |
| Colon | introducing direct speech, lists, or explanations |
| Period | end of sentence, except at end of poetic verses |
| Exclamation mark | indicating strong emotion or commands |
| Double exclamation mark | expressing extreme emphasis or astonishment |
| Question mark | ending direct inquiries |
| Combined ?! | showing rhetorical questions or wonderings |
| Double curled quotation marks (" ") | setting off direct speech or quotations |
| Single curled quotation marks (' ') | highlighting special terms or phrases |
| Em dash | used to mark a strong logical break where the second part explains, specifies, results from, or sums up the first, such as explanatory, consequential, conditional, summative, or identifying. It is attached to the preceding word and followed by a space. The em dash is used only when the break has strong added value; otherwise, a comma is preferred. |

### Speech Formatting

#### Inline Speech
Inline speech integrated within narrative sentences is enclosed in quotation marks. No em dash or colon is used before the speech. A colon before the quotation is used only when the quoted material is a complete statement; otherwise, no colon is used.

#### Block Speech
When speech is presented on a new line, the preceding line ends with a colon. The speech itself begins on a new line with an em dash (—) and is enclosed in quotation marks.

#### Multi-Paragraph Speech
Speech that spans multiple paragraphs begins each paragraph with an em dash (—) and is enclosed in quotation marks. The quotation marks are closed at the interruption and reopened in the new paragraph.

#### Nested Speech
Quotes or thoughts within other speech are enclosed in single quotation marks.

#### Mixed Narration and Speech (Verse/Passage)
In single verses or short passages where narration and speech alternate line by line, speech lines begin with an em dash (—) and are enclosed in quotation marks. Narration lines are not preceded by an em dash. Inline speech within narration lines remains enclosed in quotation marks.

#### Cold-Called Speech
Cold-called speech (spoken or thought without a preceding attribution phrase) is formatted like block speech. It begins on a new line with an em dash (—) and is enclosed in quotation marks.

#### Use of ?!
The combined question mark and exclamation point (?!) is used only in cases of intense emotional rhetorical questions where a standard question mark is insufficient to convey the full emotional force. The surrounding narrative context must clearly indicate that the question is not a genuine inquiry. This mark is applied sparingly and deliberately.

### Non‑Poetic Elements

Instructions and non-poetic elements, such as psalm titles (e.g., "מזמור לדוד", "למנצח") and the term "סֶלָה" (Selah), must be rendered in bold and on separate lines. These elements are considered external or performance/liturgical instructions, not integral parts of the poetic text itself.

### Divine Name

In non-biblical texts, the divine name must be written יהוה and not ה or יי and without diacritics.
