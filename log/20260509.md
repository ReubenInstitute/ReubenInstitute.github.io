# 9 May 2026 – DeepSeek 3 – Reading cycles document completed

_Documentation_

What began as a single Four‑Week Method page became a complete family of reading cycles, each precisely defined as an open‑source mathematical problem.  The session spanned conceptual design, editorial decision‑making, and the final assembly of a permanent `ReadingCycles.md` resource for the Institute.

### From one method to a family of cycles

The original `FourWeekMethod.md` described a single schedule built on letter‑counts.  That schedule remains the anchor of the document, but the Institute’s later work produced two additional problems: the same 4‑week cycle recast with audio durations, and a 6‑week scholarly study cycle with a split Psalm 119 and a weekday‑only structure.  The three cycles share the same transparent, constraint‑based philosophy, so they belong in a single document.

We renamed the page to **ReadingCycles.md** and gave it a universal introduction that speaks of “cycles” in the plural, not of a single method.

### Document structure and introduction

The introduction was minimally edited to remove any reference to a specific cycle.  It still explains that each cycle is the solution to a precisely defined optimisation problem, that the constraints are the “source code,” and that the Institute publishes one endorsed instance among many possible solutions.

A new **Traditional Cycle** section describes the standard 30‑day division and its severe imbalance, providing context for why the Institute’s mathematical approach exists.

### The audio‑based 4‑Week Cycle

Under a new **Duration‑based cycles** heading, the first entry is the 4‑Week Cycle re‑expressed with audio durations from `durations.csv` instead of letter‑lengths.  It is explicitly the same problem — same book‑cycling rule, same Shabbat‑double constraint, same psalm‑count caps — but with a metric that reflects actual speech time.  No published solution table is available yet; the problem is defined and ready for any solver.

### The 6‑Week Study Cycle

This cycle required the most design work.  It is a weekday‑only cycle (Sunday–Thursday full days, Friday half day, Shabbat empty) that divides Psalm 119 into two parts so that no day demands an extended session.

**Psalm 119 split** — We decided to split Psalm 119 into two parts, each a whole number of consecutive stanzas.  The initial working split is 11 + 11 stanzas, but the solver may adjust to 12 + 10 (or another division) if it yields a smaller final spread.  The individual stanza durations are provided in the CSV file.

**Duration categories** — All psalms were categorised by their production audio length:
- **Long** (> 5 min): `18, 37, 68, 69, 78, 89, 105, 106` — each must occupy a full day alone.
- **Medium** (2½–5 min): a list of 38 psalms — exactly two fit per full day.
- **Short** (under 2½ min): the remainder, used as filler inside each day’s book‑cycling sequence.

The two halves of Psalm 119 are even longer but are treated as fixed anchors in the final week.

**Pre‑populated table** — We built a 6×6 table that marks the editorial starting state:
- Psalm 1 opens the first Sunday.
- The eight long psalms sit on Wednesdays and Thursdays of weeks 2‑5 (two per week, alternating).
- The two halves of Psalm 119 occupy the last Wednesday and Thursday (week 6).
- Psalm 150 closes the final Friday.
- The 38 medium psalms are distributed exactly two per day across the remaining 19 full‑day slots (all Sundays–Tuesdays plus Thursday of week 1).
- Fridays (except the final one) are deliberately left empty of medium or long psalms, keeping them short.

This arrangement reflects the Institute’s editorial preference: avoid heavy loads at the start or end of the week, place the core study on mid‑week days, and let each week close gently.

**Formal problem statement** — A `#### Problem` block mirrors the style of the letter‑count cycle, stating the goal (minimise the maximum daily audio‑duration spread), the fixed placement of anchors, the pairing of medium psalms, the distribution of short psalms, the book‑cycling rule, and the day‑type constraints.

### Underlying data

Both audio‑based cycles share a single data file, `durations.csv`, which lists every unit together with its duration in seconds: Psalms 1–150, with Psalm 119 split into its 22 individual stanzas (119.1–119.22).  The CSV is introduced once under the duration‑cycles heading and linked from the document.

The session ended with the document structurally complete — three cycles, three problem definitions, one data file, and a clear separation between the open mathematical problems and the Institute’s recommended instance.  The 6‑week solution table will be added when the solver produces a concrete result.
