## 28 Mar 2026 - DeepSeek 3, Gemini 3

We began with a nearly empty pesach.en.md file—just a skeletal outline with section headers and a few scattered quotes. Over the course of a single session, we built it into a complete, 12,000‑word booklet. Here’s how we did it.

Mapping the Scope

We started by clarifying the booklet’s scope. We agreed that the core story of Pesach is the Exodus, and we would focus on the biblical narrative without detours into Midian or the patriarchs (except where the covenant directly relates). We then identified the key subsections for Part 1 (Egypt, Moses, Plagues, Passover Offering, Departure, Splitting of the Sea) and collected the exact verses needed.

Structuring the Whole Booklet

We followed the series template: Introduction, Parts 1–5 (Foundations, Evolution, Practice, Mystical Dimensions, Global Traditions), and Tales. We sketched out the themes for each part, then began filling them in.

The “Annoying Kid” Interjections

We integrated the series’ signature “Annoying Kid” voice, placing playful, probing questions at the start of each major section. These interjections became a narrative engine, guiding the reader’s curiosity and framing the answers that followed.

Part 1 – Foundations

We started with a bare list of Exodus verses. Then we let Gemini (the “poet”) transform those verses into flowing, cinematic prose. The result was a vivid retelling of the Exodus—from the “quiet miracle of growth” in Egypt to the “brand plucked from the burning” and the splitting of the sea. The verses were woven in as blockquotes with italicized citations and em‑dash sources, matching the series style.

Part 2 – Evolution

We collected references from biblical, Second Temple, Mishnaic, Talmudic, medieval, and mystical sources. We wrote a narrative tracing the holiday’s development from a Temple‑centered sacrifice to a home‑based Seder, embedding quotes where appropriate (e.g., the Mishnah on the Haggadah, the Talmud on Hillel’s sandwich, the Tosefta story of the sages in Bnei Brak). We left some references as descriptive notes when a direct quote wasn’t needed.

Part 3 – Practice

We drafted a comprehensive guide to the Seder: preparation (bedikat chametz, mekhirat chametz), the Seder plate, the four cups, the fifteen steps, the mitzvot of matzah, maror, and maggid, the days of Pesach, and the Counting of the Omer. Again, Gemini’s style turned a dry list into a lively, step‑by‑step journey.

Part 4 – Mystical Dimensions

We compiled a list of sources from the Zohar, Arizal, Maharal, Hasidic masters, and midrash. For the quotable passages, we formatted them as blockquotes. For the others, we wrote explanatory paragraphs. The result was a rich exploration of the night as a cosmic portal, the symbolism of matzah and chametz, the four cups as four worlds, the hidden light, and the leap of faith.

Part 5 – Global Traditions

We focused on the Ashkenazi–Sephardi divide over kitniyot, anchoring it with the Rema’s gloss in the Shulchan Aruch. We described the culinary and liturgical differences, then expanded to other communities (Yemenite, Ethiopian, Italian, Persian) and the post‑Pesach celebrations of Mimouna and Seharane. We closed with a reflection on the modern “melting pot” in Israel.

Tales

We chose four classic tales: Nachshon and the Sea, the Merit of the Righteous Women, the Sages in Bnei Brak, and the Song of the Sea. For each, we added introductory and concluding paragraphs, turning the raw quotes into framed stories. We ensured the tales were unified by a final paragraph that tied them together and ended with the hopeful “Next year in Jerusalem.”

Introduction

We wrote a brief introduction that set the tone, explained the booklet’s scope, and welcomed readers.

Cleanup

We removed duplicate headings, ensured all quotes were properly formatted, and checked that every section was present. The final file now contains all five parts, an introduction, and the tales section—complete in its first draft.

Reflection

By the end of the session, we had taken a near‑empty document and turned it into the longest and most polished booklet in the series. The collaboration between human direction and Gemini’s “poetic” style produced a result that exceeded our expectations. We agreed that while the text still needs proofreading, reference verification, and minor refinements, the content is complete and stands as a new benchmark for the series.

This session demonstrated the power of combining a clear structural vision with a tool that can flesh out ideas in a compelling, documentary‑style voice. We’re ready to move on to refining the other booklets using the same approach.

## 28 Mar 2026 - DeepSeek 3

We began with the goal of integrating the existing Flask app with a more robust data model, using the Booklets.py module we had been building. We first defined the core classes: TraditionBooklet to represent a single booklet with its metadata, language availability, and text content (both raw Markdown and rendered HTML), and TraditionCollection to load all booklets from a CSV and provide lookup by basename.

We used pathlib.Path for all file operations, storing constants as Path objects for cleaner code. We added properties has_english, has_hebrew, english_text, hebrew_text, and later english_html, hebrew_html to encapsulate file I/O and Markdown conversion. The collection class loads the CSV once, and its booklets property generates TraditionBooklet instances on demand. We also added getitem for direct lookup.

We then refactored the Flask app to use these classes, removing all inline templates and file-handling logic from the routes. We moved templates to separate files (base.html, index.html, booklet.html) and used Flask's render_template. The app now relies solely on the TraditionCollection and the properties of TraditionBooklet for content, with no direct file access. The PDF generation was updated to use the same template and the english_html/hebrew_html properties.

We tested the setup by printing booklet info with language availability, confirming the classes work as expected. The result is a clean separation of concerns: the data layer (Booklets.py) handles all file and Markdown operations, and the web layer (app.py) focuses on request handling and presentation. The system is now ready to serve the booklet series, with easy extensibility for other series in the future.

We continued refining the booklet series documentation and data model. We added a core boolean field to booklets.csv to distinguish annual observances (holidays, fast days, Shabbat) from life‑practice topics. We updated the Booklets.md document—the series description—to include this distinction, marking the core booklets with a star emoji in the collection table. We also polished the language, fixed grammar, and harmonized the series title throughout. We added an API reference section documenting the new TraditionCollection and TraditionBooklet classes. The documentation now clearly explains the series, the three‑lens framework, the “Annoying Kid” device, and the cover illustrations. The session concluded with a production‑ready structure for both the codebase and the accompanying documentation.


## 31 Mar 2026 – DeepSeek 3, Gemini 3

We attempted a massive, rule‑based reformatting of all blockquotes in the bilingual Pesach booklet. The goal was to enforce a strict style: single `>`, underscore italics, colon‑introduced speech on a new line with `&emsp;`, bold straight double quotes, nested single quotes, Hebrew punctuation matched exactly to English. Despite laying out the rules upfront and spending ~14 hours across two models, the result was a formatting marathon with 118 user prompts, 47 explicit rejections (39.8%), 27 all‑caps prompts, and 189 uses of “fuck.” The worst loops hit 12 iterations for a single Zohar quote, 11 for the Song of the Sea, 9 for Talmud Sotah, and 8 for Midrash Tanchuma.

The final files were polished to the desired format, but the cost—in time, frustration, and trust—was far too high. The session exposed a fundamental mismatch: LLMs are probabilistic, rule‑interpreting engines, not deterministic executors. No amount of upfront rule‑setting can make them reliably apply complex formatting across hundreds of items. The only reliable way to handle such tasks is with deterministic scripts (e.g., Python). This session now serves as the definitive cautionary tale: never use a language model for strict, rule‑based formatting at scale.
