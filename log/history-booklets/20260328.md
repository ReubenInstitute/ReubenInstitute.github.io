# 28 Mar 2026 – DeepSeek 3, Gemini 3 – Pesach booklet first draft completed

_HistoryBooklets_

We began with a near‑empty `pesach.en.md`—a skeletal outline with headers and a few scattered quotes—and ended the session with a complete, 12 000‑word first draft.  The workflow we discovered here became the template for every booklet that followed.

### Mapping the scope

We agreed to focus strictly on the Exodus narrative, avoiding detours into Midian or the patriarchs except where the covenant directly connected.  With the biblical boundaries set, we identified the key subsections for Part 1—Egypt, Moses, Plagues, Passover Offering, Departure, Splitting of the Sea—and collected the exact verses needed.

### The five‑part structure

The series template was applied: Introduction, Parts 1–5 (Foundations, Evolution, Practice, Mystical Dimensions, Global Traditions), and Tales.  Themes for each part were sketched, then filled in.  The “Annoying Kid” interjections—playful, probing questions placed at the head of every major section—became the narrative engine, driving curiosity and framing the answers that followed.

### Part 1 – Foundations

A bare list of Exodus verses was handed to Gemini, whose role was to function as a poetic renderer.  The result was flowing, cinematic prose: the “quiet miracle of growth” in Egypt, the “brand plucked from the burning,” the splitting of the sea.  Verses were woven in as blockquotes with italicised citations and em‑dash sources, matching the house style exactly.

### Parts 2–5

The same pattern held.  For Evolution, we gathered references spanning biblical, Second Temple, Mishnaic, Talmudic, medieval, and mystical sources, then wove them into a narrative tracing the holiday’s transformation from Temple sacrifice to home‑based Seder.  Practice became a comprehensive guide to the Seder—preparation, the plate, the four cups, the fifteen steps, the Omer.  Mystical Dimensions drew from Zohar, Arizal, Maharal, and Hasidic masters, framed as blockquotes where verbatim and explanatory paragraphs where not.  Global Traditions anchored on the Ashkenazi–Sephardi kitniyot divide, then expanded to Yemenite, Ethiopian, Italian, and Persian communities, closing with Mimouna and Seharane.

### Tales and cleanup

Four classic tales were chosen—Nachshon, the Righteous Women, the Sages in Bnei Brak, the Song of the Sea—and each was framed with introductory and concluding paragraphs that turned raw quotes into finished stories.  A unifying final paragraph tied them together and closed with “Next year in Jerusalem.”

Duplicate headings were removed, quotes were checked for formatting, and every section was confirmed present.  The final file contained all five parts, an introduction, and the tales section—the longest and most polished booklet in the series, and the new benchmark for everything that would follow.
